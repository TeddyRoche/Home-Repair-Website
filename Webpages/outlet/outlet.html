Outlet Repair

First you need to figure out what type of outlet your need.
Below are a few common types you can find in your house:

Duplex

GFCI

AFCI

USB

A common electrical receptacle featuring two side-by-side sockets for connecting two devices simultaneously. Found in homes and businesses, it provides easy access to electrical power, accommodating a variety of appliances and devices. Safety features may include tamper resistance and Ground Fault Circuit Interrupter (GFCI) protection.

A safety device designed to protect against electrical shocks. GFCIs monitor the flow of electrical current and quickly cut power if they detect imbalances, which could indicate a ground fault. They are commonly found in areas where water is present, like bathrooms, kitchens, and outdoor outlets, ensuring electrical safety in potentially hazardous locations.

A safety device designed to prevent electrical fires. AFCIs monitor electrical circuits for abnormal arcing, which can create sparks and ignite fires. If they detect dangerous arc faults, AFCIs swiftly cut power to prevent potential fire hazards. They are typically used in bedrooms and living spaces to enhance electrical safety.

A specialized electrical receptacle equipped with built-in USB ports, designed for directly charging electronic devices without the need for a separate adapter. USB outlets are a convenient addition to homes and public spaces, offering a streamlined way to power smartphones, tablets, and other USB-powered gadgets while freeing up standard outlets for other uses.

specialized electrical outlet for electric stoves and ovens. Typically rated at 240 volts with 40 or 50 amperes, it provides the high power needed for cooking appliances. Range outlets have distinctive configurations, including three or four prong slots, for safety and proper voltage supply.

Designed for electric clothes dryers, this outlet is rated at 240 volts and 30 amperes, accommodating the heating element's power requirements. Like range outlets, dryer outlets have either three or four prong slots, ensuring safe and efficient electrical connections.

Two-prong outlets are characterized by their two slots, typically one narrower and one wider. They lack a grounding slot, which means they do not provide a dedicated path for excess electrical current to safely dissipate in the event of a fault or short circuit. This absence of grounding can increase the risk of electrical shock. Two-prong outlets are often found in older homes built before modern electrical codes required grounded outlets, and they are considered less safe than their three-prong counterparts.

Three-prong outlets have three slots: two vertical slots for prongs (hot and neutral) and a semi-rounded grounding slot. This additional grounding slot allows for a dedicated path to ground excess electrical current, significantly enhancing safety. The presence of grounding reduces the risk of electrical shock and helps prevent electrical fires. Three-prong outlets are the modern standard in residential and commercial electrical systems, complying with current building codes and safety requirements.

The amperage rating of an outlet should match the amperage rating of the circuit it's connected to. Using a 20-amp outlet on a 15-amp circuit is generally acceptable, but using a 15-amp outlet on a 20-amp circuit is not safe.
When installing or replacing outlets, it's crucial to adhere to local electrical codes and safety guidelines to ensure proper and safe electrical connections for your specific needs.
